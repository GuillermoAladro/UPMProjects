MATLAB CODE EXPLANATION
Sampling, Aliasing, Interpolation, Decimation, and Multirate Signal Processing

This package contains three MATLAB scripts and one MAT data file:

1. MuestreoSC.m
2. interpYdiez.m
3. CambioTasaMuestreo.m
4. voz1_8khz.mat

The scripts illustrate the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, aliasing, discrete-time spectra, interpolation, decimation, and audio sample-rate conversion.


============================================================
1. MuestreoSC.m
============================================================

PURPOSE

This script generates a 1 kHz cosine signal and samples it at two different sampling frequencies. Its main purpose is to compare correct sampling with undersampling and to demonstrate aliasing.

SIGNAL DEFINITION

The angular frequency is:

    w = 2000*pi rad/s

The corresponding ordinary frequency is:

    f0 = w/(2*pi) = 1000 Hz

Therefore, the cosine signal completes 1000 cycles per second and has a period of:

    T = 1/f0 = 1 ms

A very small time step, Tc = 10 microseconds, is used to create a dense time-domain representation that approximates a continuous-time signal:

    xc(t) = cos(w*t)

FIRST SAMPLING CASE: fs1 = 10 kHz

The first discrete-time sequence is generated using a sampling frequency of 10 kHz:

    xn1[n] = cos(w*n*Ts1)

Because 10 kHz is greater than twice the signal frequency, the Nyquist condition is satisfied:

    fs1 > 2*f0
    10000 Hz > 2000 Hz

The script plots:

- The continuous-time cosine signal during its first 5 ms.
- The sampled sequence during the same interval.
- The magnitude of its FFT between -pi and pi radians per sample.

The sampled sequence correctly represents the original 1 kHz signal.

SECOND SAMPLING CASE: fs2 = 800 Hz

The second sequence uses a sampling frequency of only 800 Hz.

This frequency does not satisfy the Nyquist condition:

    fs2 < 2*f0
    800 Hz < 2000 Hz

As a result, aliasing occurs. The discrete samples appear to represent a frequency different from the original 1 kHz signal.

For this case, the script also plots:

- The original continuous-time signal.
- The undersampled discrete-time sequence.
- The FFT magnitude between -pi and pi radians per sample.

The comparison shows how an insufficient sampling rate changes the apparent frequency of the sampled signal.

OPTIONAL AUDIO PLAYBACK

The final sound commands are commented out. They may be enabled to play the two sequences with their correct sampling rates:

    sound(xn1,fs1)
    sound(xn2,fs2)

The first one is heard as the original 1 kHz tone. The second one demonstrates the audible effect of aliasing.


============================================================
2. interpYdiez.m
============================================================

PURPOSE

This script studies interpolation and decimation of a sampled cosine signal. It compares basic sample insertion or removal functions with MATLAB functions that also apply low-pass filtering.

The script requires MATLAB's Signal Processing Toolbox.

ORIGINAL SIGNAL

A 1 kHz cosine signal is sampled at:

    fs = 12 kHz

Since 12 kHz is greater than 2 kHz, the original signal satisfies the Nyquist criterion.

The script first displays:

- The first 10 ms of the sampled signal.
- Its magnitude spectrum between -pi and pi radians per sample.
- Its magnitude spectrum on a frequency axis expressed in hertz.

The FFT is shifted with fftshift so that zero frequency appears at the center of each spectrum.

------------------------------------------------------------
A. INTERPOLATION FROM 12 kHz TO 24 kHz
------------------------------------------------------------

The desired sampling frequency is 24 kHz. The conversion ratio is:

    24/12 = 2

Therefore, the interpolation factor is p = 2.

UPSAMPLE METHOD

The command:

    x1p = upsample(xn,p)

inserts p - 1 zeros between every pair of original samples. For p = 2, one zero is inserted after every original sample.

This operation increases the sequence length and the nominal sampling frequency, but it also creates spectral images. The inserted zeros do not reconstruct the intermediate signal values.

The script plots:

- The first 10 ms of the zero-inserted sequence.
- Its spectrum in normalized angular frequency.
- Its spectrum in hertz.

INTERP METHOD

The command:

    x1n = interp(xn,p)

also increases the sampling rate by p. Unlike upsample, interp applies a low-pass interpolation filter after inserting the zeros.

The filtering suppresses the spectral images and estimates meaningful values between the original samples.

The script plots the interpolated sequence and its spectra so that it can be compared with the output of upsample.

MAIN DIFFERENCE

- upsample only inserts zeros.
- interp inserts zeros and then applies a low-pass interpolation filter.

------------------------------------------------------------
B. DECIMATION FROM 12 kHz TO 6 kHz
------------------------------------------------------------

The desired sampling frequency is 6 kHz. The conversion ratio is:

    6/12 = 1/2

Therefore, the decimation factor is q = 2.

DOWNSAMPLE METHOD

The command:

    x2p = downsample(xn,q2)

keeps one sample out of every q2 samples and discards the others. For q2 = 2, every second sample is retained.

Because the 1 kHz input tone is below the new Nyquist frequency of 3 kHz, this particular signal can be reduced to 6 kHz without aliasing.

The script plots the downsampled sequence and its spectra.

DECIMATE METHOD

The command:

    x2n = decimate(xn,q2)

applies a low-pass anti-aliasing filter before reducing the number of samples.

This is safer for general signals because frequency components above the new Nyquist limit are attenuated before samples are removed.

MAIN DIFFERENCE

- downsample only removes samples.
- decimate applies an anti-aliasing low-pass filter and then removes samples.

------------------------------------------------------------
C. DECIMATION FROM 12 kHz TO 1.5 kHz
------------------------------------------------------------

The desired sampling frequency is 1.5 kHz. The conversion ratio is:

    1.5/12 = 1/8

Therefore, the decimation factor is q = 8.

The new Nyquist frequency is:

    fs4/2 = 750 Hz

The original signal frequency is 1 kHz, which is higher than 750 Hz. Consequently, directly reducing the rate with downsample produces aliasing.

DOWNSAMPLE BY 8

The command:

    x3q = downsample(xn,q3)

removes samples without filtering. The 1 kHz component folds into the new frequency range and appears at an incorrect frequency.

DECIMATE BY 8

The command:

    x3n = decimate(xn,q3)

filters the sequence before reducing the sampling frequency. Since the 1 kHz tone lies above the new Nyquist frequency, the anti-aliasing filter strongly attenuates it.

This comparison demonstrates that anti-alias filtering avoids frequency folding, although components outside the valid output bandwidth cannot be preserved.

OUTPUT OF THE SCRIPT

For each processing method, the script generates:

- A stem plot of the time-domain sequence.
- An FFT magnitude plot in normalized angular frequency.
- An FFT magnitude plot in hertz.


============================================================
3. CambioTasaMuestreo.m
============================================================

PURPOSE

This script converts a recorded speech signal from an initial sampling frequency of 8 kHz to a new sampling frequency of 44.1 kHz.

The script compares:

- Conversion in one rational resampling stage.
- Conversion in three smaller rational resampling stages.

It requires MATLAB's Signal Processing Toolbox.

INPUT FILE

The script loads:

    voz1_8khz.mat

The MAT file must contain an audio signal stored in a variable named:

    x

If the file also contains a variable named fs_x, that value is used as the input sampling frequency. Otherwise, the script assumes:

    fs1 = 8000 Hz

The command:

    x = S.x(:)

forces the audio signal to be a column vector, which gives consistent behavior during processing.

ORIGINAL AUDIO DISPLAY

A time vector is created from the sample indices and the 8 kHz sampling frequency.

The script plots the original speech waveform as amplitude versus time.

------------------------------------------------------------
A. ONE-STAGE SAMPLE-RATE CONVERSION
------------------------------------------------------------

The target sampling frequency is:

    fs2 = 44100 Hz

The rational conversion factor is:

    fs2/fs1 = 44100/8000 = 441/80

The command:

    [P,Q] = rat(fs2/fs1)

produces:

    P = 441
    Q = 80

The conversion is performed with:

    xc2 = resample(x,P,Q)

Conceptually, this operation interpolates by 441 and decimates by 80. MATLAB's resample function also applies the required low-pass filtering efficiently, without explicitly creating an extremely large zero-filled intermediate sequence.

------------------------------------------------------------
B. THREE-STAGE SAMPLE-RATE CONVERSION
------------------------------------------------------------

The same total ratio is factored as:

    441/80 = (9/4)*(7/4)*(7/5)

The conversion is therefore performed in three stages:

    xcb1 = resample(x,9,4)
    xcb3 = resample(xcb1,7,4)
    xcb5 = resample(xcb3,7,5)

The total output sampling frequency is still 44.1 kHz.

A multistage design can reduce computational complexity because each filter works with smaller interpolation and decimation factors.

TIME-DOMAIN COMPARISON

The script displays the first 30 ms of both converted signals:

- xc2: one-stage conversion.
- xcb5: three-stage conversion.

The two signals should be very similar, although small numerical and filter-design differences may be present.

FREQUENCY-DOMAIN COMPARISON

The script calculates FFTs for:

- The original 8 kHz speech signal.
- The one-stage 44.1 kHz result.
- The three-stage 44.1 kHz result.

The magnitudes are converted to decibels using:

    20*log10(abs(X)+eps)

The small value eps prevents the logarithm of zero.

The original spectrum is shown from -4 kHz to 4 kHz. The output spectra are shown using the Nyquist range of the 44.1 kHz output signal.

RMS ERROR

The script calculates the root-mean-square difference between the one-stage and three-stage outputs:

    error_rms = sqrt(mean((xc2-xcb5).^2))

A small RMS error indicates that both conversion strategies produce nearly equivalent results.

OPTIONAL AUDIO PLAYBACK

The final commands are commented out. They can be enabled to listen to:

- The original speech signal at 8 kHz.
- The converted speech signal at 44.1 kHz.

4. voz1_8khz.mat
============================================================

This is a MATLAB data file containing the speech signal used by CambioTasaMuestreo.m.

Expected variable:

    x       Audio sample vector

Optional variable:

    fs_x    Original sampling frequency

The file must remain in the same folder as CambioTasaMuestreo.m unless a different path is specified in the load command.


============================================================
IMPORTANT MATLAB FUNCTIONS USED
============================================================

cos
    Generates the cosine signals.

plot
    Displays continuous curves or densely sampled signals.

stem
    Displays discrete-time samples.

fft
    Calculates the discrete Fourier transform.

fftshift
    Moves the zero-frequency component to the center of the spectrum.

rat
    Approximates a sampling-rate ratio with two integers.

upsample
    Inserts zeros between samples without interpolation filtering.

interp
    Increases the sampling rate and applies interpolation filtering.

downsample
    Removes samples without anti-alias filtering.

decimate
    Applies anti-alias filtering and then reduces the sampling rate.

resample
    Performs rational sample-rate conversion with filtering.

sound / soundsc
    Plays a sequence as audio at a specified sampling frequency.


============================================================
MAIN CONCEPTS DEMONSTRATED
============================================================

1. Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem

A signal with maximum frequency fmax must be sampled at a frequency greater than twice fmax to avoid aliasing.

2. Aliasing

When the sampling frequency is too low, high-frequency components are represented as incorrect lower frequencies.

3. Interpolation

Interpolation increases the sampling frequency. Proper interpolation includes low-pass filtering after zero insertion.

4. Decimation

Decimation decreases the sampling frequency. Proper decimation includes anti-alias filtering before sample removal.

5. Rational sample-rate conversion

A conversion by P/Q combines interpolation by P, low-pass filtering, and decimation by Q.

6. Multistage processing

A large rational conversion can be divided into smaller stages to improve computational efficiency.
