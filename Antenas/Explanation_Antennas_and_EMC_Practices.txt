EXPLANATION OF THE ANTENNAS AND EMC LABORATORY PRACTICES
==========================================================

This document summarizes and explains the purpose, procedure, main results, and key lessons of
the four laboratory practices in the Antennas and Electromagnetic Compatibility course. The
simulations are carried out mainly with FEKO using full-wave electromagnetic models, and they
are complemented with analytical calculations and, in some sections, MATLAB.

The practices follow a natural progression. Practice 1 introduces numerical analysis and basic
wire antennas. Practice 2 studies multi-element wire structures such as arrays and Yagi
antennas. Practice 3 focuses on printed microstrip antennas. Practice 4 deals with high-
directivity aperture antennas: horns and parabolic reflectors.


PRACTICE 1. WIRE ANTENNA ANALYSIS
=================================

General objective
-----------------

The first practice introduces antenna analysis using the method of moments and FEKO. Its purpose
is to learn how to model thin conductors, choose a suitable discretization, and relate the
simulation results to the concepts of impedance, current distribution, radiation, near field,
far field, and bandwidth.

The operating frequency is 23 MHz, so the wavelength is approximately 13.04 m. This value is
used to define all antenna dimensions as fractions of the wavelength.

Influence of the mesh
---------------------

The method of moments divides each wire into small segments. Accuracy depends on segment size:
a very coarse mesh reduces computation time, but it may represent the current distribution
incorrectly and produce errors in impedance and radiation patterns.

Several meshes are compared, from segment lengths of approximately λ/4 down to λ/50. The study
is carried out first with a half-wave dipole and then with an electrically long dipole. For the
half-wave dipole, the results converge as the mesh is refined. With only a few segments, the
sinusoidal current distribution is represented too crudely. When the segment length is reduced
to values around λ/20 or λ/50, the differences between successive refinements become small.

The problem is more evident for the long dipole because its current distribution contains
several maxima and minima. A coarse mesh cannot reproduce all the current lobes correctly.
Therefore, a fine mesh is selected for the remaining simulations.

Field regions
-------------

The second part distinguishes three electromagnetic regions:

- Reactive region: stored energy dominates and the fields decrease rapidly with distance.
- Fresnel region: radiation exists, but the pattern still depends on distance.
- Fraunhofer region: the angular shape of the pattern no longer changes, and the fields behave
  approximately as a plane wave.

A half-wave dipole and a 1.55λ dipole are analyzed. The radial electric-field component is mainly
associated with reactive or induction terms, so it decreases faster than 1/r. The transverse
components Eθ and Hφ contain the radiation term and, in the far field, decrease approximately as
1/r. Their ratio approaches the intrinsic impedance of free space, approximately 377 Ω.

The long dipole requires a greater distance to reach the far-field region because its maximum
dimension is larger. This shows that the boundary between near field and far field depends both
on frequency and on the electrical size of the antenna.

Design of basic antennas
------------------------

A dipole close to λ/2 is adjusted until its input impedance is almost purely real. The resonant
length is slightly shorter than half a wavelength because the conductor has a finite diameter.
The final design has an input resistance close to 72 Ω and a gain of approximately 2.1 dBi.

A monopole over a perfect conducting plane is then designed. According to image theory, its
input resistance is approximately half that of the dipole, while its gain is about 3 dB higher
because the radiation is concentrated in one half-space. The resulting height is close to
0.233λ, with a resistance of about 36 Ω and a gain of approximately 5.14 dBi.

Compact monopoles are also studied, including a capacitively loaded monopole, an L-shaped
antenna, and an inverted-F antenna. These solutions reduce physical size, but they generally
increase stored reactive energy and reduce bandwidth. Finally, loop antennas are analyzed to
observe how impedance, current distribution, and radiation pattern change with electrical size
and geometry.

Main lesson
-----------

The practice shows that an electromagnetic simulation is reliable only when the discretization
is appropriate. It also relates electrical size, current distribution, impedance, directivity,
and bandwidth. Compact antennas can preserve a useful radiation pattern, but usually at the
cost of reduced bandwidth.


PRACTICE 2. DIPOLE ARRAYS AND YAGI ANTENNA
==========================================

General objective
-----------------

The second practice extends the analysis to structures with several elements. It studies an
eight-element linear dipole array using a Dolph-Chebyshev distribution and a four-element Yagi
antenna.

The objective is to apply the concepts of mutual coupling, impedance matrices, S-parameters,
array factor, sidelobe control, beam steering, directivity, and front-to-back ratio.

Dolph-Chebyshev array
---------------------

The array consists of eight parallel half-wave dipoles separated by 0.4λ. Each element has an
independent feeding port.

The first step is to calculate mutual coupling. The current in each dipole depends not only on
its own excitation voltage, but also on the fields generated by the other elements. This effect
is represented through a mutual impedance matrix. The S-parameter matrix of the complete array
is also obtained and converted into an impedance matrix with MATLAB.

A Dolph-Chebyshev distribution is then applied to obtain a controlled sidelobe level. In this
case, the design uses a 37 dB ratio between the main lobe and the sidelobes. The excitation
amplitudes are symmetric: the central elements carry the highest currents, and the excitation
decreases toward the ends. The required feeding voltages are calculated with V = Z·I, thereby
compensating for mutual coupling.

Radiation pattern, tolerances, and beam steering
------------------------------------------------

The design is checked in terms of directivity, main-beam width, and sidelobe level. Sensitivity
to ±5% frequency variations is also studied. When frequency changes, electrical lengths, mutual
impedances, and phase relationships also change, so the radiation pattern degrades.

To steer the beam, a progressive phase shift is introduced between elements. This allows the
main beam direction to change without physically moving the array. However, steering away from
the broadside direction may increase sidelobes and modify beamwidth.

Yagi antenna
------------

The Yagi antenna is built progressively using a folded dipole as the driven element, two
directors, and one reflector. The directors promote forward radiation, while the reflector
reduces backward radiation. Their lengths and spacings are optimized to satisfy the design
requirements simultaneously.

The final design achieves approximately:

- Directivity: 10.41 dBi.
- Front-to-back ratio: 18.41 dB.
- Input impedance: 90.8 Ω, almost purely real.

The folded dipole helps obtain a suitable input resistance and partially compensates for the
effect of the parasitic elements.

Bandwidth
---------

Three conditions must be satisfied at the same time: directivity above 9 dB, front-to-back ratio
above 15 dB, and VSWR below 2. The resulting operating interval is approximately 22.714 to
23.284 MHz, corresponding to 0.571 MHz or 2.48% of the center frequency.

The most restrictive condition is the front-to-back ratio. At the band edges, backward
radiation increases, while directivity and impedance matching still retain some margin.

Main lesson
-----------

In a multi-element antenna, the elements cannot be analyzed independently. Mutual coupling
modifies both currents and impedances. Radiation-pattern control requires the adjustment of
amplitudes and phases, and functional bandwidth may be limited by a radiation requirement rather
than only by the reflection coefficient.


PRACTICE 3. MICROSTRIP ANTENNAS
===============================

General objective
-----------------

The third practice designs a rectangular microstrip patch antenna fed by a coaxial probe at
1.523 GHz. Two substrates are compared: FR4 and a thicker low-permittivity dielectric.

The objective is to study how the substrate affects patch dimensions, feed position, matching,
bandwidth, efficiency, and radiation patterns.

Design method
-------------

The same procedure is followed in both cases:

1. Analytical calculation of the approximate patch width and length.
2. Calculation of the effective permittivity.
3. Correction for fringing fields.
4. Estimation of the input resistance and probe position.
5. FEKO simulation.
6. Parameter sweeps of patch length and feed position.
7. Adjustment of the S11 minimum at 1.523 GHz.
8. Calculation of bandwidth and E-plane and H-plane patterns.

The analytical calculation provides a starting point, but electromagnetic optimization is still
required because closed-form models do not include every effect associated with the feed and
fringing fields.

FR4 design
----------

FR4 makes it possible to obtain a compact antenna because of its relatively high permittivity,
but it stores more energy inside the dielectric and introduces significant losses. The design
is correctly matched at 1.523 GHz, with a reflection minimum close to -28 dB and a bandwidth of
approximately 32 MHz for S11 below -10 dB.

The theoretical efficiency is limited by dielectric losses and is estimated at approximately
57.7%.

Low-permittivity design
-----------------------

The second substrate has lower permittivity and greater thickness. As a result, the patch must
be larger, but the fields extend more into the air and less energy is stored in the dielectric.

The bandwidth increases to approximately 142 MHz. In addition, dielectric losses are much
smaller and the calculated efficiency approaches 100%.

Radiation patterns and polarization
-----------------------------------

Both designs radiate mainly in the broadside direction, perpendicular to the patch. The H-plane
pattern remains relatively regular, while the E-plane shows more variation, especially with the
thicker substrate. The copolar component is dominant and the cross-polar component remains lower,
confirming the expected linear polarization.

Main lesson
-----------

There is a fundamental design trade-off. High permittivity reduces antenna size, but it tends to
reduce bandwidth and efficiency. Low permittivity and greater substrate thickness increase the
physical dimensions, but improve bandwidth and reduce losses.


PRACTICE 4. APERTURE ANTENNAS
=============================

General objective
-----------------

The fourth practice studies high-directivity antennas at 9.046 GHz. It includes the design of a
pyramidal horn and a one-meter parabolic reflector fed by that horn.

The practice relates aperture geometry to gain, beamwidth, phase error, and efficiency. It also
studies the importance of the feed phase center and the effect of lateral feed displacement.

Pyramidal horn
--------------

The horn begins with a rectangular waveguide and expands progressively in the E and H planes.
Its dimensions are calculated with universal design curves and tables that relate aperture size,
phase error, and field level.

The complete antenna is then simulated in FEKO, obtaining Cartesian and polar patterns, E-plane
and H-plane cuts, principal planes, and symmetry planes. The theoretical design predicts
11.04 dBi, while the simulation gives approximately 11.44 dBi. The 0.40 dB difference is small
and validates the approximations used.

Parabolic reflector
-------------------

The horn is used to illuminate a one-meter reflector. The reflector converts the spherical wave
generated by the feed into an almost planar wavefront, producing a narrow beam and high
directivity.

The feed position must be defined using its phase center, not simply the physical center of the
horn aperture. The calculations indicate that the phase center lies approximately 2 to 3 mm
behind the aperture. Three positions are compared: no correction, a correction of about 2.4 mm,
and an overcorrection of about 4.8 mm.

The corrected position improves phase coherence across the reflector surface and therefore
improves gain and main-beam quality.

Symmetry and feed displacement
------------------------------

With the feed centered, the structure has two symmetry planes, so only one quarter of the full
geometry needs to be simulated. When the feed is shifted laterally, one symmetry plane is lost
and the computational cost increases.

Lateral displacements of 56.38 mm, 112.76 mm, and 169.14 mm are studied. Moving the horn shifts
the main beam, allowing beam steering without rotating the entire reflector system. However,
large displacements introduce aberrations and may reduce gain, increase pattern asymmetry, and
raise sidelobe levels.

Main lesson
-----------

Aperture antennas achieve high directivity through a large radiating surface with controlled
phase. Horn design curves provide useful approximations, but in a reflector system the phase-
center position is critical. At microwave frequencies, an error of only a few millimeters can
noticeably affect performance.


GENERAL CONCLUSION
==================

The four practices cover the main antenna types studied in the course:

- Practice 1 establishes the numerical and physical foundations of wire-antenna analysis.
- Practice 2 introduces radiation-pattern control with several coupled elements.
- Practice 3 studies printed antennas and the effect of the dielectric substrate.
- Practice 4 analyzes aperture systems intended to produce narrow beams and high gain.

Together, they show that antenna behavior depends on geometry, materials, feeding, the
electromagnetic environment, and frequency. Practical antenna design requires a combination of
theory, simulation, optimization, and critical interpretation of the results.
